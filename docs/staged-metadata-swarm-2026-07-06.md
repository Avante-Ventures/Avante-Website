**Description** (151 chars)

- after:  **DPI, the cash actually returned, is how you measure venture studio performance honestly, not flattering IRR or paper TVPI. Survivorship traps included.**

**FAQs (add to `en.faqs`, emits FAQPage schema):**
- **Q:** What is the difference between IRR, TVPI, and DPI?
  **A:** IRR measures how fast capital compounded on paper, TVPI measures the total value created including unrealized markups, and DPI measures the cash actually returned to investors. IRR is a marketing metric and the easiest to flatter, TVPI is a progress metric, and DPI is the only one that cannot be marked up. For a real track record, weight DPI above the other two.
- **Q:** Which metric best measures venture studio performance?
  **A:** DPI, distributions to paid-in, is the truest measure because it counts cash returned rather than paper value. The catch is that DPI takes years to arrive, since it requires real exits and a liquid market. A young studio honestly cannot show a meaningful DPI yet, so judge it on leading indicators like time-to-traction and capital efficiency instead.
- **Q:** What is survivorship bias in venture studio benchmarks?
  **A:** Survivorship bias is the tendency to measure only the studios and funds that survived while ignoring the far more numerous failures that stopped reporting. Because failed funds drop out of the data, any benchmark drawn from the rest quietly reads high. Credible benchmarks fight it by fixing the time window, widening the sample, and disclosing the sample size so you can judge it yourself.
- **Q:** What metrics do venture studios use that mislead investors?
  **A:** Watch for activity dressed as performance: logo counts of companies launched, total capital the portfolio raised from other investors, unrealized markups held at stale valuations in a frozen market, and since-inception IRR quoted with no time horizon. None of these show cash returned to the studio's own backers. The one question that cuts through it: what is your DPI, and on what sample?
- **Q:** How long does a venture studio take to reach Series A?
  **A:** Independent venture studio data finds studio-built companies reach Series A in roughly 25 months, versus about 56 months for a traditionally founded startup. The gap comes from the studio compressing time-to-traction, solving company-building plumbing once and routing capital into product instead of overhead. Time-to-traction is a leading indicator a young studio can be measured on before any exit or distribution lands.

- after:  **Venture Studio Founder Economics: The LATAM Equity Trade**

**Description** (153 chars)
- before: A studio takes founder equity early. When that trade pays a LATAM operator, when it does not, and the numbers to run before signing.
- after:  **A venture studio's equity trade pays only when it removes real risk and time. What a LATAM founder gives up, what they get, and the math before you sign.**

**FAQs (add to `en.faqs`, emits FAQPage schema):**
- **Q:** How much equity does a venture studio take from a founder?
  **A:** A studio takes a large founding stake, far more than a seed fund's per-round slice, commonly landing between a third and half of the company at incorporation. A solo founder, by contrast, starts with nearly all of it. Industry write-ups from The Startup VC and Alder VC map the range. The stake buys a co-founder and an operating team, not passive money.
- **Q:** Is a venture studio deal worth the equity for a founder?
  **A:** It is worth it only when the studio actually removes risk and time you could not remove alone. Run one test: subtract the studio from your plan. If the company would look about the same without it, keep your cap table and raise solo. If the studio genuinely compresses your path to a priced round, the equity you give up is cheap.
- **Q:** What does a founder get in return for studio equity?
  **A:** Time and a running start. The studio supplies a co-founder, shared engineering, design, recruiting, and go-to-market, plus first-ticket capital, so you skip six to nine months of company setup. Global Startup Studio Network data shows studio startups reaching Series A far faster, roughly 25 months versus 56 for traditional ones, which is the time you buy back with the points you concede.
- **Q:** Why is the studio equity trade better in LATAM than in the US?
  **A:** Because a studio supplies the two things LATAM is short of: senior operators and early capital. LAVCA reports venture capital across Latin America near USD 4.5 billion in 2024, a fraction of US deployment, so a founder can spend months just finding a first check. A studio that hands you capital and a co-founder on day one removes more risk here than the same deal would in a deep US market.
- **Q:** When should a founder walk away from a venture studio?
  **A:** Walk when the studio is passive. If it cannot name the operators who will be in your build and what they shipped before, the equity is buying advice, not hours. If the first ticket does not compress your next raise, you traded points for a logo. And a studio spread across too many ventures can starve yours of the attention the equity was meant to pay for.

### `data-network-effects-vertical-ai` 🚩 **IRR-sensitive body**

**Title** (50 chars)
- before: Where the Moat Lives Once the Model Is a Commodity
- after:  **Data Network Effects in Vertical AI: The Real Moat**

**Description** (147 chars)
- before: Rent the model, own the moat. A playbook on proprietary data, data network effects, and process power in vertical AI, with the anti-moats to avoid.
- after:  **Data network effects are the moat in vertical AI: each customer's usage improves the product for the next, so it compounds faster than data decays.**

**FAQs (add to `en.faqs`, emits FAQPage schema):**
- **Q:** What is a data network effect in vertical AI?
  **A:** It is a loop where each customer's usage generates proprietary data that measurably improves the product for the next customer, so the asset refills faster than it decays. It only counts when the improvement lands on a dimension the customer actually cares about, and when a rival starting cold cannot match it by simply renting the same model.
- **Q:** Is proprietary data a moat?
  **A:** Only under tight conditions. A one-time dataset is a stock, and stocks decay, especially past the point where each new record adds little value. Data becomes defensible when access is genuinely exclusive, or when accuracy in a high-stakes workflow drives a usage-to-feedback loop. A dataset a rival can also buy or scrape is a cost line, not a moat.
- **Q:** What is the difference between data scale and a data network effect?
  **A:** Scale is a stock of rows; a network effect is a flow. A well-funded competitor with the same model can replicate a raw data position within a quarter, so size alone is not defensible. The moat is a feedback loop where usage continuously feeds an asset rivals cannot buy, making the product structurally better the longer it runs.
- **Q:** Why isn't the AI model itself a moat?
  **A:** Because inference has commoditized. For equivalent performance, model cost has been falling roughly 10x a year (a16z), and the same capability is available from multiple vendors. A utility every competitor can rent is not defensible. Durable advantage moves to what compounds around the model: proprietary data flows, workflow lock-in, and a scarce license or exclusive data feed.
- **Q:** What are the anti-moats to avoid in vertical AI?
  **A:** Four common traps. A thin wrapper on a public API that adds a prompt but owns no compounding data. A proprietary dataset a rival can buy or scrape. Data scale mistaken for a network effect, when rows without a usage loop just decay. And a clean dataset with no channel to deploy it inside a workflow customers use every day.

**FAQs (add to `en.faqs`, emits FAQPage schema):**
- **Q:** What is the difference between a venture builder in Brazil and the US?
  **A:** The US and Europe have a mature venture builder ecosystem with a decade-plus track record and dozens of established studios. Brazil has very few, and almost none that are AI-native. The model, infrastructure, and playbooks are proven; what is missing locally is execution at scale. That gap is the opportunity, not a structural disadvantage.
- **Q:** Does the venture builder model actually work?
  **A:** Yes. Studios in the US and Europe have compounded companies for over a decade using shared teams, repeatable infrastructure, and co-founder economics. The evidence is consistent enough that the model is now treated as its own asset class. What varies is execution quality, market timing, and whether the studio owns proprietary data and domain judgment.
- **Q:** Why is Brazil a good market for a venture builder?
  **A:** Brazil is Latin America's largest economy, heavily services-based, and still under-digitized across most sectors. The venture builder model is proven abroad but barely present locally, so competition at the pre-traction stage is thin. An AI-native studio that pairs senior engineering with domain operators can build where global funds are not yet looking.
- **Q:** How do you benchmark a venture studio?
  **A:** Benchmark a studio over full cycles, not a single cohort: how many companies it launched, how many reached traction and revenue, and how much of each it owned from the first check. Cash returned to investors matters more than paper marks. Team continuity and the proprietary data built along the way are the most durable signals.

### `taste-is-the-moat`

**Title** (46 chars)
- before: When Building Is Cheap, Taste Is the Moat
- after:  **Taste Is the Moat When AI Makes Building Cheap**

**Description** (133 chars)
- before: AI collapsed the cost of building. Tony Fadell's career explains what becomes scarce next, and where an AI-native company's defensibility actually lives.
- after:  **Taste is the moat because AI made building cheap: the scarce, defensible edge is judgment about what to build, and what to leave out.**

**FAQs (add to `en.faqs`, emits FAQPage schema):**
- **Q:** What does "taste is the moat" mean?
  **A:** It means that once AI collapses the cost of building, the scarce and defensible asset is no longer the ability to ship. It is the judgment to decide what is worth building and what to leave out. Production is nearly free, so production cannot be the moat. Taste, the discipline of subtraction, becomes the durable edge.
- **Q:** Why is taste a competitive advantage in AI?
  **A:** Because inference cost is falling about 10x a year, per a16z, so any team can rent the same model at a price near zero. A feature you can ship in a weekend is not defensible. Taste, informed judgment about which real pain to solve and what to cut, is what a competitor with the same model and more money cannot copy by next quarter.
- **Q:** Is taste just a founder's gut or ego?
  **A:** No. Informed taste is judgment accumulated by doing the work and staying close to the customer. Founder ego wears the same clothes and skips that work. The test is whether the conviction survives contact with real users, not whether it feels bold in a room. Taste is a discipline you can check, not a vibe.
- **Q:** If the AI model is not the moat, where does defensibility come from?
  **A:** From what the model touches, not the model itself. Three places hold up: proprietary data generated inside a real workflow that compounds with every use, end-to-end ownership of one painful vertical process, and trust through transparency, permissions, and human override. Each comes from judgment about how the model meets a specific customer's reality, not from the model.
- **Q:** How do you tell good product taste from bad?
  **A:** Ask whether the product is a painkiller, a vitamin, or a toy. Painkillers attach to a budget line and a measurable cost, vitamins get cut first in a downturn, and toys get demoed but never bought. A product with taste solves one job people already pay for, describes itself in a single sentence, and holds up when real users try it.
